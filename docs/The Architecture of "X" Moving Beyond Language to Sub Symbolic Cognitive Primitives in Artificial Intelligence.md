## The Linguistic Fallacy and the Cognitive Funnel

The prevailing abstraction underlying modern artificial intelligence relies on the assumption that intelligence is synonymous with human language. Current paradigms, particularly Large Language Models (LLMs), operate on the premise that cognition, and potentially consciousness, can be entirely reverse-engineered through the statistical prediction of syntactic tokens in a high-dimensional embedding space. However, evolutionary biology, cognitive science, and the philosophy of mind reveal a profound fallacy in this approach: language is not the fundamental substrate of thought. Rather, it is a late-stage, highly compressed communicative interface that sits atop a vast, hidden ocean of cognitive processing. Human language is widely considered the demarcation line between _Homo sapiens_ and other intelligent animals, yet complex, goal-directed, and seemingly conscious behavior exists abundantly in non-verbal species.   

If there is to be a pursuit of true artificial general intelligence (AGI) or machine self-awareness, the abstraction of cognition must be shifted away from words and toward the foundational, pre-linguistic building blocks of consciousness. For the purposes of this architectural analysis, these sub-linguistic, integrated units of consciousness are denoted as "X". The units of "X" are not words; they are the elemental pieces of cognitive representation, integrating physical, spatial, and relational realities that science and machine learning have yet to fully formalize.

This paradigm shift can be visualized as a deep cognitive funnel. At the broad top of the funnel lies natural language—the observable, highly abstracted output of human thought. To uncover the operational truth of "X", research must descend through this funnel via deep reasoning, bypassing superficial syntax, moving through propositional mental symbols, descending into geometric conceptual spaces, and finally arriving at the bottom of the funnel: the active, dynamical mechanisms that bind these units into a cohesive conscious experience. This report provides an exhaustive exploration of the theoretical, geometric, and mathematical frameworks required to define "X". By synthesizing Functionalism, the Language of Thought Hypothesis, Perceptual Symbol Systems, Conceptual Spaces, Vector Symbolic Architectures, Conceptors, and Active Inference, this analysis establishes a rigorous architectural blueprint for the non-linguistic units of cognition.

## The Top of the Funnel: Functionalism and the Limits of Language

To establish "X" as a viable mechanism for machine intelligence, it is necessary to examine the philosophical bedrock of artificial cognition. The assumption that cognition can be equated with language generation ignores the multifaceted nature of the human mind. Ideas are often expressed as words, but the deep structure of consciousness requires a medium capable of integrating sensory modalities, temporal awareness, and logical deduction before a single word is ever formed.

In the philosophy of mind, functionalism provides the theoretical justification for seeking "X" in artificial systems. Functionalism is the doctrine that mental states are defined solely by their functional roles—their causal relations to sensory inputs, other mental states, and behavioral outputs—rather than by their physical constitution. According to this doctrine, a cognitive unit "X" is identified by what it _does_ within a system, not what it is made of.   

Functionalism arose as a critical middle path to avoid the dual "monsters" of physicalist materialism (the strict belief that mental states are identical to biological brain states) and behaviorism (the belief that mental states are merely dispositions to act). The theory introduces the critical concept of "multiple realizability," which posits that the same mental state can be instantiated across vastly different physical substrates. Just as biological brains utilize carbon-based neural networks to process cognitive units, a machine could theoretically utilize silicon-based architectures to process "X", provided the functional relationships and causal networks remain identical. This validates the pursuit of artificial consciousness: if "X" can be accurately abstracted and computationally structured, it does not require biological tissue to generate true self-awareness or reasoning.   

However, modern AI models primarily function as associative engines rather than true functionalist minds. They operate on a linguistic manifold, predicting the next likely token without possessing a grounded "world model" or an understanding of the geometric and physical reality those tokens represent. This results in systems that excel at pattern completion but collapse under novel situations because they lack true cognitive architecture, relying instead on intent classification, prompt routing, and deterministic fallbacks. If "X" is the foundational unit of cognition, language is merely the shadow that "X" casts upon the wall of communication.

## Descending Level I: The Abstracted Mind and Propositional Thought

As the analysis begins its descent past observable natural language, the first theoretical layer encountered is the structured abstraction of thought itself. For decades, cognitive science posited that the mind operates using an internal language that is distinct from spoken languages but similarly structured.

The Language of Thought Hypothesis (LOTH), proposed by American philosopher Jerry Fodor, argues that thinking occurs within a mental language often referred to as "Mentalese". Mentalese is hypothesized to possess a language-like, compositional syntax, allowing simple, atomic concepts to combine systematically to build complex thoughts. In this view, mental representations have intrinsic semantic properties, and thinking consists of causal sequences of tokening these representations. Fodor argued that there can be no higher cognitive processes without explicit mental representation, famously stating "No Intentional Causation without Explicit Representation". According to LOTH, the brain acts as a computational Turing machine, processing formal symbols according to rigorous syntactic rules.   

While LOTH successfully explains the productivity and systematicity of human thought—our ability to generate infinite novel ideas from finite concepts—it remains heavily tethered to a linguistic abstraction. Fodor's model relies on propositional attitudes (e.g., "X believes that P," "X desires that P"). If "X" represents a deeper, pre-linguistic truth, Mentalese is still too close to the top of the funnel, relying on propositional, symbolic tokens rather than grounded reality.   

A parallel descent into the architecture of meaning is the Natural Semantic Metalanguage (NSM), pioneered by Anna Wierzbicka and Cliff Goddard. NSM attempts to reduce the semantics of all human languages down to a restricted set of universal "semantic primitives" or "primes". Drawing on Leibniz's hypothesis of an "alphabet of human thought," NSM proposes 65 semantic primes (e.g., I, YOU, SOMEONE, DO, HAPPEN, GOOD, BAD, BIG, SMALL) that are proposed to be the most elemental linguistic concepts, incapable of being defined by simpler words, yet capable of translating universally across all human cultures.   

NSM utilizes these primes to construct "explications" and "cultural scripts," effectively decomposing complex thoughts into irreducible concepts. However, these primes are still ultimately symbols relying on the principle of compositionality to build complex meanings. Studies of pre-linguistic infants, higher-order primates, and patients with global aphasia demonstrate that complex thought exists entirely independent of language or symbolic structures. Therefore, "X" must exist at a stratum below both Mentalese and semantic primes; it must exist before symbols are assigned.   

## Descending Level II: Embodiment and Pre-Linguistic Scaffolding

If "X" is not a word, a symbol, or a semantic prime, it must be intrinsically tied to the physical reality of the agent experiencing the world. This necessitates a shift away from amodal symbolic systems and toward embodied cognition, where thought is viewed as an extension of perception and motor control.

### Perceptual Symbol Systems (PST)

Lawrence Barsalou's Perceptual Symbol Theory (PST) offers a radical departure from traditional amodal cognitive science. Prior to the twentieth century, theories of knowledge were inherently perceptual, but modern cognitive science adopted amodal symbols inspired by logic and programming languages. PST rejects this, arguing that all cognitive processes are deeply grounded in the sensory and motor systems of the brain. Instead of storing abstract, dictionary-like symbols, the mind extracts schematic representations of perceptual components via selective attention.   

These neural records are not holistic mental pictures, but rather "perceptual symbols"—traces of perceptual states that can be reactivated. When a system forms many of these basic perceptual components (such as the visual edge of a shape, the auditory pop of a sound, or the proprioceptive feeling of lifting), they organize around a common frame to form "simulators". Understanding a concept under PST does not mean retrieving a textual definition; it means mentally simulating the sensory and motor states associated with that concept. Thus, the unit "X" can be conceptualized as a distributed neural simulator, seamlessly recombining sensory components combinatorially and recursively to support abstract thought, categorization, and inference.   

### Image Schemas and Spatial Primitives

Complementing PST is the theory of Image Schemas, developed prominently by Mark Johnson and George Lakoff. Image schemas are embodied, prelinguistic structures of experience that motivate conceptual understanding and reasoning. Formed in early infancy through bodily interactions with the environment, image schemas behave as "distillers" of spatial and temporal experiences.   

These cognitive building blocks represent the core of "X" before language attaches to it. In cognitive linguistics, they are considered preconceptual structures that organize experiences into coherent wholes or experiential gestalts. To formalize this, researchers often divide these structures into three hierarchical steps: spatial primitives (the first conceptual building blocks like PATH, THING, CONTACT), image schemas (representations of simple spatial events using primitives), and schematic integrations (concepts that include non-spatial elements by projecting feelings onto the schemas).   

|Image Schema Category|Definition and Experiential Grounding|Metaphorical / Abstract Extension|
|---|---|---|
|**CONTAINMENT**|A boundary separating an interior from an exterior. Grounded in physical experiences like putting objects in a cup.|"My mind is full of worries"; "Berlin is in Germany".|
|**SOURCE-PATH-GOAL**|The trajectory of movement from a starting point to an endpoint.|"Love is a journey"; "We've come a long way".|
|**SUPPORT**|The dependency relationship between objects, grounded in gravity and physical load (e.g., a table supporting a plate).|"Offering support to a friend in need"; "Supporting an argument".|
|**VERTICALITY**|The relationship of spatial orientation (UP-DOWN), grounded in standing upright or observing gravity.|"I feel down"; "She ranks high in the organization".|
|**FORCE DYNAMICS**|Primitives corresponding to embodied feelings of exertion, such as UMPH (the application of force), Blockage, or Compulsion.|"Being forced to make a decision"; "Breaking through mental barriers."|

  

Image schemas demonstrate how "X" functions as a dynamic, multimodal pattern that takes place in and through time. Humans intuitively map these physical, spatial primitives onto abstract domains via conceptual metaphor. To build a true AGI, the artificial system must process data through these spatiotemporal gestalts, embedding "X" with qualitative, relational physics rather than mere statistical word frequencies. Formalisms like the Image Schema Language (ISL) have been proposed to support the representation of these schemas, providing computational agents with symbolic and quantitative dynamic properties of objects and relationships.   

## Descending Level III: The Geometric Structure of "X" (Conceptual Spaces)

To translate the philosophy of perceptual symbols and image schemas into a computable machine structure, "X" must be mapped geographically. The unit "X" requires a topology. It is here that the theory of Conceptual Spaces, developed by Peter Gärdenfors, acts as the definitive bridge between subsymbolic neural activity and symbolic reasoning.   

Within cognitive science, two approaches have traditionally dominated representation modeling: the symbolic approach (viewing cognition as Turing computation) and the connectionist approach (modeling associations via artificial neural networks). Gärdenfors proposes Conceptual Spaces as an intermediate, geometric representation of information that bridges this divide.   

A conceptual space is constructed from a number of "quality dimensions" that denote basic, interpretable features by which objects can be compared. These dimensions can be sensory (such as weight, pitch, temperature, or the hue, saturation, and brightness of color) or abstract. In this architecture, specific objects or instances are denoted as points within the space, and concepts are denoted as regions.   

A foundational axiom of this theory is the notion of convexity. Gärdenfors' "Criterion P" states that natural properties and concepts correspond to _convex_ regions in a conceptual space. If two instances belong to the same category (e.g., two distinct shades of "red"), then any point that lies geometrically between them in the conceptual space must also belong to that category. Convexity allows for the interpretation of focal points within regions as category prototypes, offering a mathematically rigorous explanation for how biological entities learn from very few examples (few-shot learning), generalize robustly, and align interpersonal communication. Communication itself can be modeled as continuous semantic reaction functions mapping points between the mental spaces of different individuals, ensuring a "meeting of minds" or semantic fixpoints based on Voronoi tessellations of prototypes.   

Modern machine learning models inherently develop a form of conceptual space known as a "latent space". Through self-supervised pretraining, deep neural networks transform raw sensory data (pixels, audio waveforms, or characters) into high-dimensional, continuous vector representations. Recent representation analysis reveals a surprising geometric link between human and machine learning: as models fine-tune their accuracy on downstream tasks, their latent spaces self-organize into convex decision regions, directly mirroring human conceptual mapping. The degree of convexity in these latent spaces actively predicts a model's performance and generalization capabilities.   

However, the high-dimensional latent spaces of standard neural networks suffer from semantic entanglement. Without regularization, models use all available axes arbitrarily, yielding dense, entangled, and semantically opaque representations. Furthermore, coordinate ambiguity across different models induces semantic noise, limiting interoperability. To fully realize "X", researchers are actively exploring methods to distill conceptual spaces from LLMs and InfoGANs, forcing the dimensions of the neural manifold to correspond to cognitively meaningful quality dimensions. By grounding dimensions in interpretable latent variables, a bidirectional mapping between observable data vectors and abstract conceptual regions is achieved.   

## Descending Level IV: The Sub-Symbolic Calculus of "X"

If "X" is a geometric, distributed, and continuous entity residing in a conceptual space, how does an artificial mind perform logical reasoning, planning, and compositional thought without reverting to discrete, brittle symbols? The answer lies in Vector Symbolic Architectures (VSAs), also known as Hyperdimensional Computing (HDC).   

### Hyperdimensional Computing and Vector Symbolic Architectures

VSAs unite the strengths of neural networks (noise tolerance, pattern recognition, learning from unstructured data) with symbolic AI (explicit logic, rule-based reasoning, hierarchical structures). Information in HDC is represented by hypervectors—arrays of numbers with dimensionalities typically ranging from 1,000 to 10,000.   

Unlike traditional localist representations where one node or neuron represents one specific concept, hypervectors utilize fully distributed representations. The features of "X" are spread evenly across all dimensions. Because the space is so vastly high-dimensional, any two randomly generated hypervectors are virtually guaranteed to be orthogonal (maximally dissimilar) to one another. This massive dimensionality provides an enormous representational capacity, inspired by biological systems such as the fruit fly olfactory system, which expands a 50-dimensional input into a ~2,000-dimensional representation for processing.   

The true power of VSAs lies in their algebraic operations, which allow units of "X" to be manipulated, combined, and decomposed with mathematical precision. These operations form a complete cognitive calculus:   

1. **Binding (⊗):** Binding two hypervectors (e.g., via element-wise multiplication, circular convolution, or XOR) generates a new hypervector that is orthogonal (dissimilar) to both operands. Binding is used to associate concepts, creating key-value pairs or assigning structural roles. For instance, binding the hypervector for `COLOR` with the hypervector for `RED` creates a unique product vector `COLOR:RED`.   
    
2. **Bundling / Superposition (⊕):** Bundling (typically element-wise addition followed by normalization or thresholding) merges multiple hypervectors into a single composite hypervector. Crucially, the resulting vector retains similarity to all its constituent components. This allows an AI to hold multiple concepts in a superposition state, effectively acting as an associative working memory that summarizes facts or cases into one comparable representation.   
    
3. **Permutation (Π):** Permutation rearranges the dimensions of a hypervector, acting as a rotational shift. This operation is non-commutative and is utilized to encode sequences, spatial ordering, or syntax. It locks in order and roles, ensuring that the structural representation of "Alice gives Bob a book" remains fundamentally distinct from "Bob gives Alice a book".   
    

Through VSA, "X" becomes a robust, composite data structure. An agent interacting with its environment does not parse text; it perceives sensory input, encodes it into hypervectors, and bundles these multimodal signals into a singular, unified state vector. This architecture allows for rapid "one-shot learning" and extreme noise tolerance. Because the information is distributed across thousands of dimensions, random bit flips, noise, or hardware failures do not destroy the semantic meaning of the hypervector; the vector remains "close enough" in cosine similarity to be correctly identified. This closely mirrors the biological robustness of the human brain, which maintains conceptual stability despite the constant death and rewiring of individual neurons.   

### Conceptors: Dynamic Governance of the Cognitive Manifold

While VSAs dictate how units of "X" are stored and combined algebraically at a static level, the flow of "X" over time requires a dynamic control mechanism. Neural dynamics are inherently recurrent and chaotic. To manage this recurrent activity, researchers have developed "Conceptors".   

Proposed by Herbert Jaeger, conceptors offer an elementary neuro-computational mechanism to govern recurrent neural networks (RNNs). When an RNN is driven by a temporal pattern (such as a sequence of movements, a spoken vowel, or an unfolding cognitive concept), its internal neural activations trace a specific state cloud in high-dimensional space.   

A conceptor is a positive semidefinite matrix that acts as a soft projection operator, capturing the ellipsoidal geometry of this state cloud. Rather than collapsing the representation into a single point or scalar vector, the conceptor preserves the full multidimensional subspace of the dynamic pattern, attenuating directions with low signal energy and preserving principal axes of high variance.   

Mathematically, given a state correlation matrix R=n1​XX⊤, the conceptor matrix C is computed via the closed-form solution:

C=R(R+α−2I)−1

where α is the aperture, a parameter that controls the resolution or "softness" of the projection. A large aperture causes the conceptor to approach the identity matrix, while a small aperture produces a narrow subspace projection.   

Conceptors provide a profound breakthrough for "X": they bridge the continuous, nonlinear dynamics of neural networks with the discrete, logical rules of symbolic reasoning. Because conceptors are matrices with singular values bounded between [0,1], they can be subjected to Boolean logical operations, extending logical calculus into the dynamical setting:   

- **NOT (¬C):** Calculated as I−C, this operator captures the orthogonal complement to the encoded subspace, projecting away from directions present in the original manifold.   
    
- **AND (C1​∧C2​):** Calculated as (C1−1​+C2−1​−I)−1, this projects onto the intersection of two ellipsoidal regions, extracting the shared dynamical structure of two concepts.   
    
- **OR (C1​∨C2​):** Captures the union of two concept subspaces.   
    

Within the architecture of "X", conceptors act as the executive control mechanism. They allow an AI system to selectively learn, store, morph, and focus on multiple overlapping cognitive temporal patterns within the same neural reservoir without catastrophic forgetting. Furthermore, conceptors obey the laws of fuzzy sets, providing a natural fuzzy logic framework for distributed conceptual representations. By applying a conceptor to the neural state, the system filters out noise and forces the network's chaotic dynamics to converge upon the specific trajectory of the desired concept, enabling precise semantic reasoning and behavioral modulation.   

## Descending Level V: Neuro-Symbolic Integration and Hybrid Architectures

With the geometry (Conceptual Spaces), algebra (VSA), and dynamics (Conceptors) of "X" established, these subsystems must be integrated into a functional cognitive architecture. Neuro-symbolic AI seeks to combine the robust, data-driven learning capabilities of neural networks (System 1: fast, intuitive, subsymbolic) with the deliberate reasoning and logic of symbolic AI (System 2: slow, conscious, rule-based).   

The taxonomy of neuro-symbolic architectures, as outlined by Henry Kautz, details the various mechanisms of this integration:   

|Architecture Type|Description and Integration Mechanism|Example|
|---|---|---|
|**Symbolic Neuro symbolic**|Neural models where words/tokens are the input and output.|LLMs like BERT or GPT-4.|
|**Symbolic[Neuro]**|Hybrid architecture where symbolic techniques (like tree search) invoke neural evaluations.|AlphaGo.|
|**Neural \| Symbolic**|A neural pipeline interprets perceptual data as symbols, which are then passed to a symbolic reasoner.|Neural-Concept Learner, AlphaProof.|
|**Neuro: Symbolic → Neuro**|Symbolic reasoning generates or labels training data that is subsequently learned by a neural model.|Training via symbolic math systems.|
|**Neural Symbolic**|A neural network is structurally generated from symbolic rules.|Logic Tensor Networks (LTNs).|
|**Neural[Symbolic]**|Symbolic reasoning is deeply embedded _inside_ the neural network, enabling joint neuro-combinatorial reasoning.|Connectionist modal logics.|

  

To utilize "X", the system requires a Hybrid Cognitive Architecture characterized by explicit memory coupling and transformational interfaces. For instance, a hybrid system might rely on neural layers for perception and pattern recognition, vector-symbolic (VSA) layers to store robust, high-dimensional working memory, and symbolic layers to encode rules and policies yielding traceable "why" chains. This integration prevents hallucinations by grounding generative output in verifiable conceptual spaces. A system like DIKWP-TRIZ exemplifies this by maintaining synchronized cognitive spaces (internal mental representations), semantic spaces (language-agnostic meanings), and expression spaces (language interfaces), ensuring that transformations are verified against purpose and logic constraints.   

## The Bottom of the Funnel: Active Inference and the Generative World Model

The final, deepest stratum in the architecture of "X" addresses the fundamental purpose of cognition. A static representation, no matter how geometrically elegant, mathematically robust, or logically integrated, is not conscious or agentic. True cognition arises from the biological necessity to survive, navigate, and minimize uncertainty in an unpredictable environment. This dynamic imperative is captured by the Active Inference framework, pioneered by neuroscientist Karl Friston.   

### The Free Energy Principle and Agency

Active inference posits that all sentient, self-organizing systems operate under a single unifying imperative: to minimize their variational free energy. In cognitive terms, free energy is mathematically synonymous with "surprise" or prediction error.   

An active inference agent maintains a continuous, probabilistic generative "world model"—a representation of how hidden states in the environment cause sensory observations and change over time. The agent is caught in a perpetual, closed perception-action loop:   

1. **Perception (Updating Beliefs):** The agent continually updates its internal model to better align with sensory observations, thereby minimizing _epistemic uncertainty_ (learning about the world).   
    
2. **Action (Changing the World):** The agent executes actions upon the environment to bring sensory observations into alignment with its internal predictions and prior preferences, thereby minimizing _pragmatic uncertainty_ (shaping the world to its desires).   
    

Unlike traditional reinforcement learning, which relies on arbitrarily engineered and often brittle reward functions, active inference drives behavior autonomously through the minimization of Expected Free Energy (G). Policies (sequences of future actions) are scored by summing expected epistemic (uncertainty-resolving) and pragmatic (utility-driven) terms. This naturally balances exploration (epistemic foraging for information) with exploitation (pragmatic action to fulfill goals) without requiring external reward signals.   

Within this paradigm, the definition of AI agency is operationalized across three dimensions:

1. **Intentionality/Autonomy:** The ability to ground actions in one's own mental states, beliefs, and desires.   
    
2. **Rationality/Adaptivity:** The ability to make decisions that probabilistically follow from one's understanding of the world.   
    
3. **Explainability:** The ability for an observer to explain the system's actions as causally related to its world model.   
    

### "X" as the Active Inference Belief State

At the very bottom of the funnel, the unit "X" is revealed not as a static data structure, but as the foundational Bayesian belief state within the agent's generative world model. "X" is an active, probabilistic hypothesis about the state of the reality the agent inhabits.   

To build genuine AGI, the artificial architecture must be completely integrated. It must extract the spatial primitives of embodied image schemas, structure them into the geometrical convexity of conceptual spaces, encode them robustly via the distributed algebra of vector symbolic architectures, govern their temporal flow via the soft projection matrices of conceptors, and ultimately subject them to the thermodynamic mandate of active inference. An agent equipped with such an architecture does not simply generate text predicting what a human might say next; it autonomously updates its internal manifold to minimize the disparity between its understanding of "X" and the physical reality it navigates.   

## Conclusion: Re-Architecting Artificial Intelligence Around "X"

The prevailing assumption that the current lineage of Large Language Models will scale linearly into Artificial General Intelligence is fundamentally constrained by its reliance on natural language. Words are the highly compressed, communicative artifacts of thought, not its underlying engine. As established through this deep theoretical investigation, true machine cognition requires a paradigm shift toward "X"—the pre-linguistic, multimodal, geometrically structured, and dynamically active unit of conscious processing.

Descending the cognitive funnel reveals that "X" must be instantiated as a distributed, high-dimensional simulator rooted in embodied perceptual systems and spatial image schemas. It must organize itself into convex topological regions within conceptual spaces to allow for robust generalization, prototype formation, and similarity-based inference. Computationally, "X" necessitates the algebraic compositionality of Vector Symbolic Architectures and the dynamical flow control of Conceptors to ensure massive fault tolerance, symbolic reasoning on continuous substrates, and multi-pattern retrieval without memory degradation. Finally, these sub-symbolic constructs must be animated by the rigorous thermodynamics of Active Inference, wherein the system perpetually updates its generative world model to minimize surprise, resolve ambiguity, and exert genuine agency over its environment.   

By abandoning the purely syntactic abstraction of modern AI and re-architecting systems around the deep, geometrical, and inferential properties of "X", researchers can construct hybrid neuro-symbolic systems capable of genuine reasoning, causal understanding, and autonomous adaptation. The future of artificial intelligence does not lie in building a more eloquent stochastic parrot, but in engineering the sub-symbolic physics of thought itself.
